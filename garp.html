Giving According to GARP:

An Experimental Study of Rationality and Altruism

ABSTRACT: Altruism and fairness are thought to be important in many economic situations. This paper asks whether altruistic choices can be captured in a simple neoclassical framework with well-behaved preferences for giving. We collect data with several different prices of altruism and verify that 96 percent of subjects make choices that are consistent with the Generalized Axiom of Revealed Preference. We estimate utility functions for subjects which could have generated the data, and then use these estimates to predict, successfully, data from outside our experiment. We argue that this neoclassical approach to a demand for altruism can go a long way toward understanding many anomalous results in economic laboratory experiments, and can provide an empirical foundation for theoretical models of a direct demand for giving.
