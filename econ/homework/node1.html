Introduction

We study the process of ovation that eventually takes place after a good play in a theater or after an interesting lecture. This process is essentially dynamic, and includes interaction not only between the artist or lecturer and the audience, but also within the audience. Therefore, the description of different types of agents who form an audience, and the interaction between them, conform the core assumptions of the model.

Different agents perceive the same lecture in a different way, and they also put different weights on the behaviour of their neighbours and of the whole auditorium. In general, people do not like to reveal their preferences in public, especially if they are very different from the average. That is why the decision of those who start clapping is the most difficult. They really want to be sure that they want to do it even if the whole hall remains silent. This effect produces nonlinear relationship between the real quality of lecture, and the applause, which, though observable, cannot be used as an adequate measure of the quality of the lecture or play.

In our model we try to make a distinction between simple clapping and ovation, which includes also standing up. A person, ceterus paribus, should get higher utility from the lecture or play if he/she not only claps, but also stands, and the lecture or play is perceived as very good.

Though it is possible to model just a binary decision between clapping and not clapping at each moment of time for a particular person just by considering whether some sort of utility is above a threshold point, we want to proceed in a different way. On the one hand, the threshold has been made endogenous, so to speak, for the individual compares the utility of clapping to that of not clapping, which depends on a number of arguments that vary with time. On the other, one version of our model considers mapping (in a sigmoid fashion) the utilities derived from the different actions to the probabilities that such actions are indeed chosen by the individuals. We also consider the analytics of a case in which it is the case that the reservation utility of clapping is determined outside the model.

The process of clapping also has a dynamics at the individual level intended to capture such an effect as tiredness from clapping, which also depends on overall reaction of the hall.

This model, being rather general, is a bit complicated . Thus, we make several simplifications to our general model and report computational experiments for particular specifications of some functions and parameters.
