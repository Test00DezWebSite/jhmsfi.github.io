I. Introduction

     In this research, we construct a hierarchical model of society that consists of regions and countries. For each region and country, we impose a specific network structure; i.e., for each region and country, we choose one network structure from the Barabasi-Albert model, the lattice model, the Small-World model and the Erdos-Renyi model. Figure 1 shows one of these cases: both regions and countries have the ring lattice structure.

Furthermore, some agents in each region are connected inter-regionally and internationally. Under this circumstance, we presume that there are two types of people: the rebels (five percent of the population) and the conservatives (the rest of the population). The conservatives do not wish the current government to be replaced. On the other hand, the rebels wish the current government to be replaced. Here, we make a further assumption: although conservatives do not wish the current government to be replaced, they have certain dissatisfaction, which allows them to become rebels under the right pressure of their rebel neighbors. Each agent has a threshold from 0 to .9 of the minimum proportion of neighbors that have to be rebels in order to become one of them. Agents are heterogeneous in such threshold.

Agents and the network structure are instantiated, and rebel agents are distributed randomly across the population. Then, we performed the following algorithm:

•For each conservative agent i: count the number N of rebels who were directly connected to her.

• If N/degreei > proportionThresholdi: she becomes a rebel.

•Else: she remains as a conservative.


Our calibration consisted of four countries, each of them with a random number of regions (from 10 to 15), and each region with a random number of agents (from 25 to 100). Each country was connected to one other country. The initial proportion of rebel agents was 5%. The average degree of connection between regions was four. The average degree of an agent was 10. We ran 32 experiments for each combination of network topologies, of 100 steps each one.


II. Results

     The primary result is shown in Figure 2.

     According to this result, if a region has the Barabasi-Albert network structure, regardless of the network structure of the country it belongs to, the current government will likely be replaced with the probability of .62 to .64. If a region has the Lattice network structure, the likelihood of the government change is somewhat lower than that of the Barabasi-Albert case; however, the probability is still slightly higher than .5 regardless of the network type the country it belongs to. If a region has the small-world network structure, this time the possibility of the government change is significantly lower; i.e., for all kinds of the country’s network structure, the probability of the government change is between .35 and .37. If a region has the lattice network, the result is similar to that of the small-world network. Regardless of a country’s network type, the probability of the government change is between .35 and .37.

     Secondly, Figure 3 indicates the evolution of the increase of the population of the rebels.

In this case, both the regions and the countries they belong to have the same network structure; i.e., Barabasi-Barabasi, Lattice-Lattice, etc. The result indicates that the Barabasi-Barabasi is most “vulnerable” to the social change and the small-world is the most resilient.


III. Conclusion and Discussion

     For both figures, it turns out that the network structure of Barabasi-Albert has significant effect on the government change. This is most likely because of the scale-free structure of the Barabasi-Albert network; i.e., because most of the agents have few neighbors (low degree), it only takes a few rebels to produce a proportion of rebel neighbors higher than the proportion threshold. Other kind of networks with less skewed degree distributions impose a higher requirement of rebel neighbors in order to propagate a change in the majority of the people.

     The limitation of this research is that, first, if we want to construct a realistic model of social movement of today’s world, we cannot ignore the influence of mass media and the Internet (e.g., consider various social and political movement such as the toppling of dictatorial regimes in the Middle East, the Tea Party movement, the Occupy movement, etc.). However, in this research, we did not include such influences explicitly.

     Second, we fail to effectively describe how the change in one country spreads to the other country. We examined the case that one country became dominated by the rebels and the other country had a few rebels who had direct contacts with the rebels in the country that had become dominated by the rebels. In this case, because of the low number of rebels in the other country, the government change did not occur. Consequently, our future agenda is to modify our model so that the government change spreads beyond borders.

     Despite the limitations described above, we believe our model makes some contribution to the understandings on the spread of social and political movements.
