        Big Petroleum Exporters Move to Stanch Oil Glut

          WSJ, Monday, March 23, 1998 by Bhushan Bhuree

          Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, and Mexico moved to end an oil glut and a period of cheap energy prices...

          The three major oil producers said yesterday that they had agreed to reduce their own oil production and seek cuts by others, such as Norway, Russia, and Oman. Their goal: a supply reduction of as much as 2 million barrels a day, or about 2.5% of the total world demand for oil of about 5 million barrels a day.

          In a joint statement issued in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, the oil ministers of the three countries said that they had already received pledges of cuts from other producers that added to their own reductions, amounted to 1.1 million barrels per day.

          That is seen by industry experts as more than enough to trigger a sharp turn around in oil markets, which have seen prices drop by as much as 40% since OPEC members decided to raise their output ceiling by 10% or two million barrels a day, in November...

          Oil-futures prices bounced back at the end of last week amid talk that producers were preparing to cut back supply. On Friday, crude oil for April delivery rose to $14.32, up 12% from Tuesday's low of $12,80. (Tuesday's price) represented a decline of more than 50% from prices in the winter of 1996-97.

          Sunday's accord surprised many in the industry who figured that OPEC would need more time to agree to output cuts and that (OPEC) could expect little help from other (non-OPEC) producers....

          ...During the oil-price crash of the mid-1980's, some non-OPEC countries had promised, but never delivered, cuts in their output.

        Non-OPEC Members Get Higher Profile

          WSJ, March 24, 1998 by Peter Fritsch

          The surprising agreement between the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries and other large oil producers to cut oil production may not be enough to trim the glut of world supplies. But the pact does signal a new role for non-OPEC countries like Mexico.

          The decision by Saudi Arabia, Venezuela--both OPEC members--and Mexico over the weekend to trim output to push up low oil prices won some support from other producers and sent oil prices soaring. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, crude oil for May delivery jumped $1.90 to $16.51 per barrel.

          Kuwait, Iran, and the United Arab Emirates--as expected--announced that they too will cut output to shore up world prices, as did Oman, Algeria, and Libya.

          The participation of Mexico was key to the unusual decision, analysts said. When oil prices collapsed in the mid 1980's, and again to a lesser extent, in 1993, OPEC complained that non-OPEC producers failed to act to underpin the market...

          Still it remains to be seen whether the latest round of oil-production cuts are enough. Because so many OPEC producers were exceeding their quotas, the latest agreement brings the market back to its production levels of last Thanksgiving when OPEC agreed to boost production by two million barrels a day. But a mild winter and troubles in Asia also have weakened oil demand. Longer term, new oil provinces in Central Asia, West Africa, and Latin America promise to add to supply, and advances in oil technology have made it economic to hunt for oil at lower oil prices. And one day, Iraq will again be pumping several million barrels a day--at least.

          Cut in Oil Output May Prove Less Than Expected

            WSJ, March 25, 1998 by Bhushan Bhuree

            As OPEC's oil ministers plan a meeting early next week to endorse temporary output reductions, some industry experts are already figuring the supply cuts will prove to be far less than expected.

            Major oil producers might reduce their output by only about half the announced goal of two million barrels per day, or 2.6% of world output, experts say, hampering OPEC's efforts to push crude-oil prices higher...

            Yesterday, crude oil for May delivery fell 59 cents to $15.92 per barrel., a day after it rose $1.90 following the announcement of of supply cuts...

            After adding up potential supply cuts, industry experts say the supply reductions are likely to be in the range of 750,000 barrels a day to 1.1 million barrels a day...

            [This story contains some other interesting tidbits.]

            • Oil prices could shoot up higher if Norway, the world's second-largest exporter after Saudi Arabia, agrees to reduce its output...Norway is still considering how to proceed.
            • An oil-market expert said that Russia isn't likely to reduce its production. He says that Russia in the past couldn't cut output when its industry was controlled by one state entity and that they are even less likely to be able to do so now with output decisions decentralized to many producing associations.
            • (Oil-market experts) said that producers of heavy high-sulfur oil in California and Canada and operators of low-volume stripper wells were on the verge of closing down operations before the weekend announcement. Moreover, major international oil companies were beginning to re-evaluate proposed capital spending on oil-production projects in the light of low oil prices.
